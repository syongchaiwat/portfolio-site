# From Static to Dynamic: Survival Analysis for Cashback Claim Rate Forecasting

> Replacing a static claim rate predictor with a hazard rate model — enabling fresher training data, daily-updating predictions, and a more correct logical framing of the cashback claimability timeline.

**Skills:** Survival analysis, hazard rate modeling, LightGBM, statistical decomposition, time-series prediction, chronological train/test design, ML production pipelines, model calibration

## Key Outcomes
- Decomposed the weighted claim rate into a behavior-shift component and an amount-distribution-shift component — confirming the two move largely independently (correlation ~0.24), which validated that genuine behavior changes exist and the 6-month training lag was a real problem worth solving
- Hazard rate model enables training on 60-day-old bookings (vs. 180-day minimum under the previous approach) — dramatically fresher training signal and faster adaptation to behavior changes
- Chronological train/test split outperforms random split on held-out future periods — critical for correctly evaluating a model that will be deployed into changing real-world conditions
- Identified a systematic LightGBM bias when training separate models per time step: a single combined model predicting all hazard steps outperforms per-step models by reducing over/under-prediction at specific days in the claimability window
- New model updates predictions daily as the expiry deadline approaches — eliminating the sharp cost discontinuity that occurred when bookings expired under the old static model

## Article

### Motivation

Cashback at Agoda follows a specific lifecycle: a booking earns a cashback reward 60 days after checkout, which the customer can then claim for 120 days (until day 180 post-checkout). The old claim rate model was trained exclusively on bookings where this claimability window had fully expired — meaning only bookings more than 180 days old were included in training data. The logic was straightforward: you can only know the final claim outcome (claimed or not) after the window closes. Training on anything earlier means you're learning from incomplete labels.

This design had two significant drawbacks. First, the training data was always at least six months stale. If customer claim behavior shifted — due to a product change, a notification timing update, or a shift in the customer mix — the model would take months to reflect it. Second, the model issued a single static prediction for each booking at the time of creation and never updated it. From a cost accounting perspective, this created a jarring discontinuity: when a booking reached its expiry date without being claimed, the model's claim rate prediction dropped from its static value to zero or one in a single step, producing sudden large changes in the modeled cost exposure.

Before committing to a new model, though, there was a prior question to answer: is the six-month lag actually a problem? The weighted claim rate — computed as the sum of cashback amounts claimed divided by total cashback amounts issued — is amount-weighted, not a simple average. This means observed changes in the metric conflate two very different things: genuine shifts in customer claiming behavior, and shifts in the distribution of cashback amounts. If a new product campaign pushed more high-value cashbacks, and high-value cashbacks are claimed at different rates, the aggregate metric moves — but not because customers changed their behavior. The old model, trained on historical data, would naturally absorb this kind of distributional shift over time without a fresh training signal. The staleness problem only bites when customer behavior itself changes — when people claim more or less often within a given cashback amount range. If all variation came from amount distribution shifts, the new model would provide no meaningful improvement.

To answer this, the weighted claim rate was decomposed into two components using an integral framing: one capturing within-bin claim rate changes (the behavior shift, Δ*C(a)*) and one capturing changes in the amount distribution itself (Δ*p(a)*). Measured across several years of production data, the two components showed very low correlation (~0.24) — meaning they move largely independently, and behavior does genuinely shift on its own. Events like the introduction of cashback abuse controls, changes to minimum cashback caps, and seasonal patterns each produced distinct footprints in the behavior component that were not explained by amount distribution changes. This confirmed that the training lag was a real and recurring problem — not an artifact of how the metric is constructed — and that a model capable of learning from fresher data would provide genuine value.

The hazard rate approach solves both problems by reframing the question. Instead of predicting "will this booking ever be claimed," it asks "given that this booking hasn't been claimed yet, what is the probability it will be claimed today?" This shift in framing is the core of survival analysis, and it opens up the ability to use partial observations (bookings still within their claimability window), incorporate the time dimension explicitly, and update predictions continuously as more days pass without a claim.

### Approaches

- **Decomposing the claim rate metric to validate the problem before building the solution.** Because the observed claim rate is amount-weighted, it changes whenever either customer behavior or the cashback amount distribution shifts. These are fundamentally different problems: distribution shifts are absorbed naturally by a model as it retrains on new data, while behavior shifts require fresher training signal to capture quickly. To determine which was driving observed claim rate changes — and therefore whether a new model would help — the metric was decomposed into two components. Expressing the weighted claim rate as an integral over cashback amount bins (∫*C(a)·p(a)·da*, where *C(a)* is the claim rate within bin *a* and *p(a)* is the probability density of amounts), any period-over-period change can be split into: a behavior-shift term (Δ*C(a)* — changes in claim rate within bins) and a distribution-shift term (Δ*p(a)* — changes in how cashback amounts are distributed). The two components showed low correlation (~0.24) across years of production data — confirming they move largely independently. This was the prerequisite finding: genuine behavior shifts are real and recurring, and a model with a six-month lag will consistently miss them.

- **Reframing claim rate as a survival analysis problem.** In survival analysis, the hazard rate h(d) is defined as the probability that an event occurs on day d given that it hasn't occurred on any previous day. For cashback, the event is claiming, and the days are numbered from the earn date (day 0 of the claimability window) through the expiry (day 120). The full claim rate for any booking is then the product of the complements: 1 minus the product of (1 − h(d)) across all days from 0 to 120. This formulation has a useful property: you can compute an updated claim rate estimate for any booking at any point in its claimability window, using only the days you've observed. A booking that hasn't claimed in the first 30 days has lower remaining claim probability than one that hasn't claimed in the first 3 days — and the model captures this naturally.

- **Exploded dataset design: one row per booking per day.** To train a model that predicts hazard rates, the training data must reflect the time structure. This means transforming each booking into a sequence of rows — one per day in the claimability window — where each row represents the question "was this booking claimed on this specific day, given it wasn't claimed on any previous day?" Bookings that were claimed contribute positive labels on their claim day and negative labels on all prior days. Bookings that expired without claiming contribute negative labels for their entire window. This exploded format allows a standard binary classifier to learn the hazard function. It also enables the use of partially observed bookings (60–180 days old) as training examples: their rows up to the current observation date are valid training data, even though their final outcome isn't yet known.

- **Resampling for manageable training data size.** The exploded dataset is large — each booking generates up to 120 rows. At production scale, this is computationally prohibitive without sampling. A resampling strategy based on the CDF of claim days was used: days near the expected claim timing are sampled more densely, and early days (where claim probability is very low and most labels are negative) are downsampled. This preserves the most informative part of the hazard curve while keeping training data size tractable. The sampling correction is factored into the model's training weights to prevent the sampling from introducing systematic bias in the hazard rate estimates.

- **Chronological train/test split to simulate real deployment conditions.** A critical design decision was how to split training and validation data. The naive approach — random 80/20 split — produces a validation set drawn from the same time period as training. For a model deployed to predict future bookings, this is optimistic: it doesn't test whether the model generalizes to time periods it hasn't seen. The chronological split tests exactly this: train on bookings up to a cutoff date, validate on bookings after it. In experiments with injected claim rate shifts (simulating a future change in customer behavior), the chronological split produced models that adapted significantly better to the shift than models evaluated with random splits. The practical lesson: for any model that will operate in a changing environment, validation accuracy on a random hold-out is a poor proxy for real-world performance.

- **Diagnosing and fixing systematic LightGBM bias in multi-step hazard prediction.** When the hazard model was initially structured as separate models — one LGBM model per day in the claimability window — a systematic bias emerged. The model for day 0 tended to underpredict the hazard rate, while the model for day 9 tended to overpredict. The reason is subtle: LGBM with early stopping can overfit to the specific distribution of positive labels at each day, and when the claim-day distribution is imbalanced (most claims happen in a narrow window of days), models trained on individual days can amplify small artifacts in that day's data. The fix was to train a single combined model that takes day index as a feature, alongside booking-level features. This model shares information across days, which naturally smooths the hazard curve and prevents individual days from being overfitted. The combined model produced better-calibrated hazard rates across the full 120-day window.

### Key Takeaways

- **Survival analysis is not just for clinical research — it's a natural fit for any system where the timing of an event matters.** Whenever you have right-censored data (outcomes not yet known because the observation window hasn't closed), survival analysis unlocks training on partial observations that would otherwise be excluded. For cashback, this alone cut the minimum data age for training from 180 days to 60 days. That's not a marginal improvement — it's the difference between a model that reflects last season's behavior and one that reflects last month's.

- **Reframing "will it happen?" as "will it happen today, given it hasn't happened yet?" changes what you can model.** The hazard rate formulation incorporates time explicitly, which the original claim rate model didn't. A booking that hasn't been claimed after 90 days should receive a very different prediction than one at day 5 — the hazard model handles this naturally, while a static model would require manual feature engineering to capture the same information.

- **Random train/test splits can give dangerously optimistic validation accuracy for models deployed into a changing world.** This is one of the most commonly underappreciated sources of model failure in production ML. When temporal drift is present — and in almost any real-world system, it is — the gap between random-split validation accuracy and true future performance can be large. Chronological splits are a more honest test, and the magnitude of the difference is itself diagnostic: a large gap between random and chronological accuracy is a signal that the model's features are drifting.

- **Validate the problem before building the solution — especially when the metric conflates multiple causes.** The weighted claim rate is amount-weighted, which means it responds to two independent phenomena: customer behavior and amount distribution. A new model only helps with one of them. The decomposition step was not optional analysis — it was the prerequisite that determined whether the entire modeling effort was worth pursuing. This principle generalises: any time your target metric is a weighted aggregate, ask whether a shift in the metric actually reflects the thing your model is supposed to learn, or whether it could be explained by a compositional change in the underlying data.

- **Shared models across related tasks often outperform task-specific models when data is limited or imbalanced.** The finding that one combined hazard model outperforms 11 separate per-day models is an instance of a general principle: when individual tasks share structure (here, the underlying claim probability curve is smooth across days), sharing parameters via a joint model improves regularization and reduces overfitting on any single task. The cost is slightly less flexibility per task; the benefit is substantially better generalization.
