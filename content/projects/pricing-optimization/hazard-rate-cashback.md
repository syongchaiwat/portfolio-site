# From Static to Dynamic: Survival Analysis for Cashback Claim Rate Forecasting

> Replacing a static claim rate predictor with a hazard rate model — enabling fresher training data, daily-updating predictions, and a more correct logical framing of the cashback claimability timeline.

**Skills:** Survival analysis, hazard rate modeling, LightGBM, statistical decomposition, time-series prediction, chronological train/test design, ML production pipelines, model calibration

## Key Outcomes
- Decomposition of the weighted claim rate confirmed there were distinct historical periods where the behavior-shift component alone drove the majority of claim rate change — proving the training lag was a real accuracy problem, not a theoretical one
- Hazard rate model enables training on 60-day-old bookings (vs. 180-day minimum under the previous approach) — dramatically fresher training signal and faster adaptation to genuine behavior changes
- Instead of predicting hazard rates at all 120 distinct claimable days, bookings are grouped into ~10 information-equal bins — preserving predictive accuracy while significantly reducing inference time
- New model correctly attributes cashback cost across the full booking lifecycle: the old model consistently overestimated cost by over 20% during the claimable period, because it held claim rate predictions constant for unclaimed bookings while updating claimed ones to 1.0 — inflating the aggregate cost estimate until expiry

## Article

### Motivation

Cashback at Agoda follows a specific lifecycle: a booking earns a cashback reward 60 days after checkout, which the customer can then claim for 120 days (until day 180 post-checkout). The old claim rate model was trained exclusively on bookings where this claimability window had fully expired — meaning only bookings more than 180 days old were included in training data. The logic was straightforward: you can only know the final claim outcome (claimed or not) after the window closes. Training on anything earlier means learning from incomplete labels.

This design had a critical drawback: the training data was always at least six months stale. But staleness only matters if customer claim behavior actually changes. If all variation in the observed claim rate came from shifts in the cashback amount distribution — for example, a campaign that pushed more high-value cashbacks — the old model would naturally absorb this through its training data, because the new distribution would eventually dominate the historical data it learns from. A new model with fresher data would not help here.

The staleness problem is only real when customer behavior itself shifts — when people claim more or less often within a given cashback amount range. To determine whether this had happened historically, the weighted claim rate was decomposed into two independent components: one driven by behavior changes, and one driven by amount distribution changes. The analysis revealed distinct periods in production data where the behavior-shift component alone was the primary driver of claim rate changes — when cashback abuse controls were introduced, when notification timing changed, and during specific seasonal patterns. These were genuine behavior events that a model trained on six-month-old data would have been slow to reflect. That confirmed the problem was worth solving.

The second problem was cost attribution. The old model issued a static prediction for each booking at creation and never updated it. For unclaimed bookings, the model continued predicting the original claim rate right up until expiry — even though a booking that goes 100+ days without claiming is very unlikely to claim at all. For claimed bookings, it updated to 1.0. This asymmetry meant the aggregate cost estimate was systematically inflated during the claimable period, overstating expected cashback liability by more than 20%. The new model solves this by updating predictions daily as the claimability window progresses.

### Approaches

- **Decomposing the claim rate metric to validate the problem.** The weighted claim rate — Σ(C_i × A_i) / Σ(A_i), where C_i indicates whether booking i was claimed and A_i is its cashback amount — responds to two distinct phenomena: changes in customer claiming behavior, and changes in how cashback amounts are distributed. Expressed as an integral over cashback amount bins (∫C(a)·p(a)·da, where C(a) is the claim rate within bin a and p(a) is the probability density of amounts), any period-over-period change decomposes into a behavior-shift term (ΔC(a) — changes in claim rate within bins) and a distribution-shift term (Δp(a) — changes in the amount distribution). Examining these components historically confirmed that behavior shifts did occur independently of amount distribution changes, and in some periods were the dominant driver. This was the prerequisite finding: it established that the training lag was a recurring accuracy problem before any modelling work was committed to.

- **Resampling for manageable training data size, with information-equal day binning.** The training dataset requires one row per booking per claimable day, which is large at production scale. A resampling strategy based on the CDF of claim days was applied: days near the expected claim timing are sampled more densely, while early days (where most labels are negative) are downsampled. The sampling correction is factored into training weights to prevent bias. Critically, rather than treating all 120 claimable days as distinct feature values, days are grouped into approximately 10 information-equal bins — days at the start of the window, when the majority of claims occur, receive their own individual bins, while the long tail toward expiry is grouped. This significantly reduces inference time: instead of combining 120 individual hazard predictions to compute a final claim probability, only ~10 are needed, while preserving the information density of the signal.

- **Reframing claim rate as a survival analysis problem.** In survival analysis, the hazard rate h(d) is the probability that an event (claiming) occurs on day d, given it hasn't occurred on any earlier day. The full claim probability for any booking is: 1 − ∏(1 − h(d)) across all claimable days. This formulation has a key property: it can be evaluated at any point in the claimable window using only the days observed so far. A booking that hasn't claimed after 90 days receives a lower remaining probability than one at day 5 — the model encodes this naturally, without manual feature engineering. It also unlocks training on partially observed bookings (60–180 days old): their rows up to the current observed date are valid training examples, cutting the minimum training data age from 180 to 60 days.

- **Chronological train/test split by observed date.** The splitting strategy is by observed date — the date at which each row of training data was generated — rather than by checkout date. This ensures the model only learns from the past to predict the future, and that the distribution of d values in training matches the real-life production distribution. In production, d=0 rows are always the most recent and most numerous; a random split would mix these with older observations and produce an artificially representative validation set that doesn't reflect deployment conditions. The observed-date split preserves the natural data distribution and prevents information leakage from future observations into training.

- **One combined model across all claimable days, with known and accepted positive bias.** Rather than training separate models per day, a single LGBM model takes d as a feature alongside booking-level and customer-level features. This approach was chosen for two reasons: it generalizes better through shared parameters, which regularize the model and reduce overfitting on any individual day; and it is significantly more maintainable in production — one model to monitor, retrain, and version rather than ten. The combined model carries a small systematic positive bias: it slightly overpredicts the overall claim rate. The reason is understood — bookings that claim early (at small d, where hazard rates are highest) do not contribute rows for larger d values, so the training data for large d is skewed toward bookings with lower propensity to claim, leading to slightly elevated predictions overall. This bias is accepted deliberately: a small, consistent overestimate of cashback cost is on the safer side for business planning, the bias is stable and well-characterised, and it is unlikely to change given the known cause.

### Key Takeaways

- **Survival analysis — reframing "will it happen?" as "will it happen today, given it hasn't happened yet?" — unlocks training on partial observations and naturally encodes the time dimension.** Whenever you have right-censored data (outcomes not yet known because the observation window hasn't closed), this framing lets you use data that would otherwise be excluded entirely. For cashback, it cut the minimum training data age from 180 days to 60 days and eliminated the need to manually engineer the time-since-earn signal — the hazard function encodes it structurally.

- **Validate the problem before building the solution — especially when your metric conflates multiple causes.** A new model only helps when the thing it's learning actually changes. For the cashback claim rate, the prediction is at booking level using everything known about the booking and the customer. If all claim rate variation came from shifts in the cashback amount distribution, model accuracy would not suffer. It is only when customer behavior genuinely shifts within a given amount range that the training lag causes errors. The decomposition analysis was the prerequisite step that confirmed this was happening, and that a fresher model would actually improve accuracy.

- **Shared models across related tasks can outperform task-specific models — and are often worth choosing even when they introduce known, bounded bias.** The combined model was preferred over separate per-day models for generalization and maintainability. The positive bias it introduces is small, understood, and stable — and because it causes the model to slightly overestimate cashback cost, it errs on the safe side. Knowing the cause of a bias and accepting it deliberately is an engineering decision.
